On Online Window Shopping

Posted on July 22, 2015

Note that this post isn't meant to be a critique of online shopping services (i.e., Amazon et al. are wonderful), but rather one on a type of behavior associated with online shopping itself.

Let me elaborate. "Online Window Shopping (OWS)." It's kind of a phrase already, and it's pretty self-explanatory. To avoid connotation misinterpretation (a post on this to come), I specifically mean to describe the long-ish term series of actions of online shopping while having a low purchasing probability. For example, think of someone who receives emails about online promotions, spends time browsing each site (without buying anything), and returns to checking the rest of their email after half an hour1.

I don't know how prevalent this behavior is, but at the very least it's extremely common among a subset of my friends. The fundamental error associated with this is a failure to make specific goals and optimize for those goals.

Further examination of this, in the context of OWS.

  1. What goals does OWS aim to accomplish?
    1. Make more informed purchasing decisions (on quality/value/getting the best deal)
    2. Actually buy things
    3. Relieve short-term boredom and/or increase happiness levels
    4. Others that I'm unaware of (comment if you think I've missed notable ones)
  2. Is OWS actually successful at accomplishing these goals?
    1. Probably not, because of diminishing marginal returns on the value of information
    2. By definition, no
    3. Maybe - this varies
  3. What alternatives would be more effective than OWS?
    1. For most common items, Amazon ratings/best sellers should more than suffice. Additionally, price comparison websites. For many other items, I would argue that buying ones of known good quality (i.e., more reliable brands) at a higher prices is often a better solution than OWS, and in any case, buying and returning may be more time efficient than OWS. However, this is dependent on income/time/flexibility/etc.
      1. For instance, spending 20 minutes looking for a coupon to save $5 off an online grocery purchase isn't worth it if you value your current time at >$15/h2.  
    2. Windowless online shopping (regular ol' OS)
    3. If OWS isn't optimal for accomplishing 1c, which I suspect is true for most people, not to worry! There are a number of alternatives, which range from taking drugs to sleeping more to journaling, again depending on the specific person.
  4. Bonus step: meta-level analysis of those goals in 1 (i.e., how important are these goals to me?)

My opinion on OWS generalizes to normal window shopping in and of itself, going to lecture, and what CFAR refers to as Garfield errors, for most use cases.

1 To be fair, I specifically might be biased against this type of behavior because of my negative views on materialism.
2 True story - I made this mistake last night, and Michael called me out on it.
